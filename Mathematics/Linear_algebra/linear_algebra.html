I - Linear Algebra to Algebraic Foundations

{% include section_navigation.html %}

The Mathematics of Structure and Symmetry

Linear Algebra is one of the most foundational areas of modern mathematics, valued by scientists and engineers for its diverse and powerful applications. Rather than focusing on manual computations — something that first-time learners often emphasize, as they have in earlier math classes — linear algebra rewards a deep understanding of concepts and structures. Large-scale numerical work is delegated to computers; what remains for the learner is the conceptual scaffolding that makes those computations meaningful and that generalizes far beyond matrices. This section follows that scaffolding from solving linear systems, through vector spaces and the spectral viewpoint, into Abstract Algebra (groups, rings, fields, polynomial rings, finite fields), and finally into the continuous symmetry of Lie groups and Lie algebras.

In the modern view, mathematics begins with the study of structure. This shift from a "science of quantity" to a study of internal rules is what allows us to bridge pure logic and high-level computation. Within the Compass, Section I plays the role of The Core: every other section inherits its language. The Discrete World (Section IV) rests on group-theoretic and field-theoretic structure; the Continuous World (Section II) rests on vector spaces, inner products, and linear operators; the stochastic models of Probability & Statistics (Section III) rest on covariance matrices, spectral decompositions, and exponential-family algebra; and the high-dimensional optimization engines of Machine Learning (Section V) rest on every one of these tools at once. Linear algebra is, in this sense, less a single subject than the common algebraic language all later sections speak.

Two outgoing paths emerge from Section I and shape much of the curriculum's forward arc. The first is a symmetry path: from the geometric symmetries of \(D_n\), \(SO(3)\), and \(SE(3)\), through the Lie group series (matrix Lie groups, the matrix exponential, Lie algebras, and the Lie correspondence), toward representation theory and ultimately the Geometric Deep Learning viewpoint in Section V — where continuous symmetry constrains neural-network architecture. The second is a discrete-algebraic path: from polynomial rings and extension fields into finite fields, which already underpin the algebraic foundations of AES, post-quantum schemes, and error-correcting codes; this path is anchored here in Section I and is planned to expand in Section IV into dedicated cryptography and coding-theory pages. By mastering these foundations, you gain the tools to navigate the rest of the Compass — and, more importantly, to recognize the same algebraic structures recurring in places they are not always named.
