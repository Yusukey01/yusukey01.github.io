Stochastic Matrix

A probability vector \(x \in \mathbb{R}^n\) is a vetor with nonnegative entries that add up to 1, and A stochastic matrix \(P \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times n}\) is a square matrix whose columns are probability vectors.

A Markov chain: is a sequence of probability vectors \(x_0, x_1, x_2, \cdots \) tohether with a stochastic matrix \(P\) such that \[ x_1 = P x_0, \quad x_2 = P x_1, \quad x_3 = P x_2, \quad \cdots. \] So, the Markov chain is explained by the first-order difference equation: \[ x_{k+1} = P x_k \quad \text{for } k = 0, 1, 2, \cdots. \] Here, \(x_k\) is called a state vector.
Example: Consider the following two states:
  • State 1: a student is sick
  • State 2: a student is not sick
We obserbed an initial state distribution: \[ x_0 = \begin{bmatrix} 0.1 \\ 0.9 \end{bmatrix} \] which means now, 10 % of students are 90 % are not.

Moreover, we assume the following conditions:
  • 70% of sick students recover the next day and 30 % remain sick.
  • 5 % of not sick students become sick the next day and 95 % remain not sick
So, our stochastic matrix can be written as \[ P = \begin{bmatrix} 0.3 & 0.7 \\ 0.05 & 0.95 \end{bmatrix}. \] Then, \[ x_1 = P x_0 = \begin{bmatrix} 0.3 & 0.7 \\ 0.05 & 0.95 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 0.1 \\ 0.9 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 0.075 \\ 0.925 \end{bmatrix} \] This means that on the next day, approximately 7.5 % students are expected to be sick and 92.5 % are not.

Steady-state Vector

A steady-state vector \(q\) for a stochastic matrix \(P\) is defined as \[ P q = q. \] In statistics, we also call it stationary distribution.

The eigenvalues of the stochastic matrix \(P\) provide insight into how fast the Markov chain converges to the stationary distribution. So, we can solve: \[ \det(P - \lambda I) = 0. \] In our example, we obtain \(\lambda_1 = 1\), and \(\lambda_2 = 0.25\). The convergence rate is governed by \(\lambda_2\).
