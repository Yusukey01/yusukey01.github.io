Ideals

In our study of group theory, we learned that we cannot simply form a factor group using any subgroup; we require a specific kind of structure called a normal subgroup. Similarly, in ring theory, to construct a new structure by "dividing" a ring, we need a special kind of subring called an Ideal.

Recall that a subring is a subset that is closed under subtraction and multiplication. An ideal is a subring with a much stronger property: it is "super-closed" under multiplication. If you multiply an element of the ideal by any element from the entire ring (even from outside the ideal), the result is "absorbed" back into the ideal. This absorption property is essentially a generalization of "multiplying by zero," and it is crucial for ensuring that multiplication is well-defined in the resulting factor ring.

Definition: Ideal

A subring \(A\) of a ring \(R\) is called a (two-sided) ideal of \(R\) if for every \(r \in R\) and every \(a in A\), both \(ra\) and \(ar\) are in \(A\).

Theorem: Ideal Test

A nonempty subset \(A\) of a ring \(R\) is an ideal of \(R\) if

  1. \(a-b \in A\) whenever \(a, b \in A\).
  2. \(ra\) and \(ar\) are in \(A\) whenever \(a \in A\) and \(r \in R\).

Factor Rings

The concept of a factor ring (or Quotient Ring) is the generalization of modular arithmetic. In basic arithmetic, we construct \(\mathbb{Z}_n\) by taking the integers \(\mathbb{Z}\) and identifying all multiples of \(n\) with zero. In the language of rings, we say we "mod out" by the principal ideal generated by \(n\), denoted \(\langle n \rangle\) or \(n\mathbb{Z}\).

We can extend this idea to any ring \(R\) and any ideal \(A\). By treating the elements of \(A\) as "zero," we collapse the ring into a simplified structure \(R/A\). The elements of this new ring are cosets — slices of the original ring shifted by the ideal. This construction is one of the most powerful tools in algebra, allowing us to manufacture new fields (like Galois Fields for cryptography) by carefully choosing the ring \(R\) and the ideal \(A\).

Prime & Maximal Ideals

Not all factor rings are created equal. If we mod out \(\mathbb{Z}\) by \(6\), we get \(\mathbb{Z}_6\), which has zero-divisors (since \(2 \cdot 3 = 6 \equiv 0\)). However, if we mod out by \(5\), we get \(\mathbb{Z}_5\), which is a perfect field. What is the fundamental difference between the ideal \(\langle 6 \rangle\) and the ideal \(\langle 5 \rangle\)?

The answer lies in the classification of ideals. Just as integers can be composite or prime, ideals can be classified based on their internal structure. This section introduces prime ideals and maximal ideals. These concepts provide the deterministic recipe for constructing specific algebraic structures: prime ideals yield integral domains, while maximal ideals yield Fields. This correspondence is the engine behind constructing the finite fields used in AES encryption and Elliptic Curve Cryptography.

Definition: Prime Ideal

A prime ideal \(A\) of a commutative ring \(R\) is a proper ideal of \(R\) such that \(a, b \in R\) and \(ab \in R\) imply \(a \in A\) or \(b \in A\).

Definition: Maximal Ideal

A maximal ideal \(A\) of a commutative ring \(R\) is a proper ideal of \(R\) such that, whenever \(B\) is an ideal of \(R\) and \(A \subseteq B \subseteq R\), then \(B = A\) or \(B = R\).

Theorem:

Let \(R\) be a commutative ring with unity and let \(A\) be an ideal of \(R\). Then \(R / A\) is an integral domain if and only if \(A\) is prime.

Theorem:

Let \(R\) be a commutative ring with unity and let \(A\) be an ideal of \(R\). Then \(R / A\) is a field if and only if \(A\) is maximal.

Ring Homomorphisms

In Structural Group Theory, we established that homomorphisms are the "structure-preserving maps" that allow us to compare different groups. We saw that the kernel of a group homomorphism is not just any subgroup, but specifically a normal subgroup.

We now extend this concept to rings. Because a ring involves two operations — addition and multiplication — a ring homomorphism is a stricter requirement: it must preserve the structure of both operations simultaneously.

This is the final piece of the puzzle justifying the definition of an ideal. Just as the kernel of a group homomorphism was a normal subgroup, the kernel of a ring homomorphism is exactly an ideal. The first isomorphism theorem for rings mirrors the one for groups, confirming that "ideals" are indeed the correct structural equivalent to "normal subgroups" in the richer landscape of ring theory.

Definition: Ring Homomorphism & Isomorphism

A ring homomorphism \(\phi\) from a ring \(R\) to a ring \(S\) is a mapping from \(R\) to \(S\) that preserves the two ring operations.
\(\forall a, b \in R\),

  • \phi(a + b) = \phi(a) + \phi(b)
  • \phi(ab) = \phi(a)\phi(b)

A ring homomorphism that is both one-to-one and onto is called a ring isomorphism.
