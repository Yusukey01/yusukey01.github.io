Graph Theory

Graph theory is a core area of discrete mathematics that focuses on the study of graphs â€” mathematical "structures" made up of vertices (or nodes) and edges that connect them. Theoretically, it provides rigorous frameworks to understand properties like connectivity, cycles, and network flows, offering deep insights into the underlying structure of complex systems. On the practical side, graph theory helps model relationships in social networks, improve recommendation systems, and it is also used in advanced techniques like graph-based machine learning models.

Graph

In mathematics, a graph is defined by \[ G = (V, E) \] where \(V\) is a finite set of vertices, denoted by \(G(V)\), and \(E\) is a set of edges, denoted by \(G(E)\). Their "sizes" (or formally cardinalities) are denoted by \(v(G) = |V(G)|\) and \(e(G) = |E(G)|\).

Each edge is an unordered pair of distinct vertices: \[ E \subseteq \binom{V}{2}. \] Also, the neighborhood of a vertex \(x\), denoted by \(N_G (x)\) (or \(N(x)\)) is is the set of vertices \(y\) such that \(xy\) is an edge, and the degree of a vertex \(x\) is defined as the number of neighborhoods of \(x\) and denoted by \(\text{deg }_G (x) = | N_G(x) |\)(or \(\text{deg }(x)\)).

For each \(A \subseteq V(G)\), we define \(e(A)\) as the number of edges with "both endpoints" in \(A\). Also, Given \(A, B \subseteq V(G)\) we define: \[ e(A, B) = | \{ (a, b) \in A \times B: ab \in E(G)\} |. \] If \(A\) and \(B\) are disjoint, this is the number of edges between \(A\) and \(B\).
