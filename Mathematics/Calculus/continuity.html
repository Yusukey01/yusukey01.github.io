Introduction

Continuity is one of the most fundamental concepts in analysis because continuity let us transfer properties such as connectivity and compactness from domain to range. In calculus, you learned that a function is continuous if "small changes in input produce small changes in output." With our metric space framework, we can now make this precise and extend it to functions between arbitrary metric spaces, not just subsets of \(\mathbb{R}\).

Why does this matter for machine learning and optimization? Neural networks are compositions of continuous functions. The loss landscape we navigate during training is (usually) continuous.

But not all notions of continuity are equal. Uniform continuity provides stronger guarantees, and Lipschitz continuity, which bounds how fast a function can change is essential for proving convergence rates of optimization algorithms.

Continuity

First, we would like to generalize \(\epsilon - \delta\) continuity to metric spaces.

Definition: Continuous Function Suppose \(X\) and \(Y\) are metric spaces, \(z \in X\) and \(f: X \to Y\). We say that \(f\) is continuous at \(z\) in \(X\) if and only if for each open subset \(V\) of \(Y\) with \(f(z) \in V\), there exists an open subset \(U\) of \(X\) with \(z \in U\) such that \(f(U) \subseteq V\).
Theorem: Suppose \((X, d)\) and \((Y, e)\) are metric spaces and \(f: X \to Y\) is continuous function. Suppose \(Z\) is any metric superspace of \((f(X), e)\). Then \(f: X \to Z\) is also continuous.
Proof: Suppose \(U\) is an open subset of \(Z\). Then \(U \cap f(X)\) is open in \((f(X), e)\) because \(Z\) is a metric superspace of \((f(X), e)\), and \(U \cap f(X) = W \cap f(X) \) fpr some open subset \(W\) of \((Y, e)\) because \((f(X), e)\) is metric subspace of \((Y, e)\). Thus, \[ \being{align*} f^{-1}(U) &= f^{-1}(U \cap f(X)) \\\\ &= f^{-1}(W \cap f(X)) \\\\ &= f^{-1}(W), \end{align*} \] which is open in \(X\) because \(f: X \to Y\) is continuous. Since \(U\) is any open subset of \(Z\), this implies \(f: X \to Z\) is also continuous.

Similarly, it is clear that compositions preserve continuity.

Theorem: Continuity of Compositions Supose \(X\), \(Y\), and \(Z\) are metric spaces and \(f: X \to Y\) anf \(g: Y \to Z\). If \(f\) and \(g\) are continuous, then \(g \circ f\) is continuous.
Proof: Suppose \(f\) and \(g\) are continuous and \(W\) is an open subset of \(Z\). Then \(g^{-1}(W)\) is open in \(Y\) and so \(f^{-1}(g^{-1}(W))\) is open in \(X\), but this set is \((g \circ f)^{-1}(W)\). Since \(W\) is any open subset of \(Z\), \(g \circ f\) is also continuous.
The topological characterization explains why continuity is really about preserving "nearness" structure, not about specific distances.

Uniform Continuity

Why isn't regular continuity enough? The \(\delta\) depends on the point.

Definition: Uniform Continuous Function Suppose \((X, d)\) and \((Y, e)\) are metric spaces, \(z \in X\) and \(f: X \to Y\). Suppose \(S \subseteq X\). Then \(f\) is said to be uniformly continuous on \(S\) if and only if for every \(\epsilon \in \mathbb{R}^+\), there exists \(\delta \in \mathbb{R}^+\) such that for every \(x, z \in S\) for which \(d(z, x) < \delta\), we have also \(e(f(z), f(x)) < \epsilon\).
Theorem: Suppose \((X, d)\) and \((Y, e)\) are metric spaces, \(S \subseteq X\), and \(f: X \to Y\).

If \(f\) is uniformly continuous on \(S\), then the restriction of \(f\) to \(S\) denoted by \[ f |_S : S \to Y \] is continuous.

Moreover, if \(f\) is uniformly continuous on \(X\), then \(f |_S \) is uniformly continuous on \(S\).

An important fact is that any continuous fuction is uniformly continuous on all compact subsets of its domain. We won't discuss compactness here, but we shall introduce the following definition of compactness. This is not the only way to describe compactness.

Definition: Completeness Suppose \(X\) is a metric space. Then \(X\) is said to be a compact metric space if and only if \(X\) is bounded and has the nearest-point property.
In numerical analysis, uniform continuity ensures that approximation errors behave consistently across the domain.

Lipschitz Continuity

A type of global continuity that we have encountered in linear maps between normed linear spaces is called Lipschitz continuity, which is uniform and preserves boundedness.

Definition: Lipschitz continuous function Suppose \((X, d)\) and \((Y, e)\) are metric spaces, and \(f: X \to Y\). If there exists \(k \in \mathbb{R}^+\) such that \[ e(f(a), f(b)) \leq kd(a, b), \, \forall \, a, b \in X, \] then \(f\) is called a Lipschitz function on \(X\) with Lipschitz constant \(k\).
Theorem: Suppose \((X, d)\) and \((Y, e)\) are metric spaces, \(S \subseteq X\), and \(f: X \to Y\).

If \(f\) is a Lipschitz function on \(S\) with Lipschitz constant \(k \in \mathbb{R}^+\), then \(f\) is uniformly continuous on \(S\) and \(\delta\) is the definition of uniform continuity can be taken to be \(\frac{\epsilon}{k}\).

Moreover, If \(f\) is a Lipschitz function on \(X\) with Lipschitz constant \(k \in \mathbb{R}^+\), \(f |_S\) is also a Lipschitz function with Lipschitz constant \(k\).

When the Lipschitz constant \(k = 1\), the Lipschitz function has a special name, isometry.

Definition: Isometry Suppose \((X, d)\) and \((Y, e)\) are metric spaces and \(\phi: X \to Y\). Then \(\phi\) is called an isometry or an isometric map if and only if \[ \forall a, b \in X, \, e(\phi(a), \phi(b)) = d(a, b). \] Moreover, the metric subspace \((\phi(X), e)\) of \(Y, e\) is an isometric copy of the space \((X, d)\).

More generally, any Lipschitz function between metric spaces that has Lipschitz constant \(k \leq 1\) is called a contraction. When \(k\) is strictly less than \(1\), we call it a strong contraction.

Definition: Strong Contraction Suppose \((X, d)\) is a metric space, and map \(f: X \to X\) is called a strong contraction on \(X\) if and only if \[ \exists k \in [0, 1) \text{ such that } \forall x, z \in X, \, d(f(x), f(z)) \leq kd(x, z). \]

Immediately, the strong contraction \(f\) is a Lipschitz function with Lipschitz constant \(k < 1\), and uniformly continuous on its domain. We will use the strong contraction later when we discuss completeness.

Lipschitz constants appear everywhere in optimization:
  • L-smooth functions (gradient is L-Lipschitz)
  • Convergence rates depend on Lipschitz constants
  • Neural network stability (spectral normalization)
