Introduction

Throughout this curriculum, we have invoked the Extreme Value Theorem: a continuous function on a closed and bounded set attains its maximum and minimum. This theorem is the reason we can speak of "optimal solutions" in constrained optimization—it guarantees they exist.

But what exactly is special about "closed and bounded"? The answer is compactness. In \(\mathbb{R}^n\), compactness is equivalent to being closed and bounded (the Heine-Borel theorem). In general metric spaces, compactness is more subtle—but equally powerful.

Compactness captures the idea that a set is "finite-like" in a topological sense: you cannot escape to infinity, and sequences cannot wander forever without accumulating somewhere. This chapter formalizes compactness and proves why it guarantees the existence of extrema.
