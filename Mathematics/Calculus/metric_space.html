Introduction

Up until this chapter, we relied on calculus and linear algebra to solve optimization problems. For many practical machine learning applications, these tools are sufficient. However, as we move toward advanced topics like manifold learning or information geometry, intuition alone is no longer enough.

We need to be able to formally answer questions such as: "Does this optimization algorithm truly converge?" or "How do we define the structure of a space when we treat entire functions as single points?" To address these, we must shift from concrete computation to mathematical analysis. Concepts like completeness and topology are not just theoretical abstractions; they are the formal requirements that ensure our optimization paths actually lead to a solution.

Metric Space

Remember, In Section I we defined the measure space as follows.

Metric Space \((M, d)\):

A metric space is an ordered pair \((M, d)\)consisting of a nonempty set \(M\) and a metric \(d\) on \(M\). The metric \(d\) is a function \(d: M \times M \to \mathbb{R}\) that defines the distance between any two elements of \(M\). For all \(a, b, c \in M\), the following axioms must hold:

  1. Positivity: \(d(a,b) \geq 0\) with equality if and only if \(a = b\).
  2. Symmetry: \(d(a,b) = d(b,a)\).
  3. The triangle inequality \(d(a,b) \leq d(a,c) + d(c, b)\).

Like the set theory, for two metric spaces \((X, d)\) and \((Y, e)\), we can say that \(X\) is a metric subspace of \(Y\) and \(Y\) is a metric superspace of \(X\) iff \(X\) is a subset of \(Y\) and \(d\) is a restriction of \(e\).

Isometries

Definition: Isometry Suppose \((X, d)\) and \((Y, e)\) are metric spaces and \(\phi: X \to Y\). Then \(\phi\) is called an isometry or an Isometric map if and only if \[ \forall a, b \in X, \, e(\phi(a), \phi(b)) = d(a, b). \] Moreover, the metric subspace \((\phi(X), e)\) of \(Y, e\) is an isometric copy of the space \((X, d)\).

Distance, Boundary

Definition: Distance Suppose \((X, d)\) is a metric space and \(A\) and \(B\) are subsets of \(X\). The distance from \(A\) to \(B\) to be \[ \text{dist }(A, B) = \inf \{d(a, b) \mid a \in A, b \in B\}, \] which depends on the metric \(d\).
Definition: Boundary Suppose \(X\) is a metric space, \(S\) is a subset of \(X\) and \(a \in X\). Then \(a\) is called a boundary point of \(S\) in \(X\) if and only if \[ \text{dist }(a, S) = \text{dist }(a, S^c) = 0. \] The collection of boundary points of \(S\) in \(X\) is called the boundary of \(S\) in \(X\) and is denoted by \(\partial S\).

Open & Closed

Definition: Open and Closed Subsets Suppose \(X\) is a metric space and \(S\) is a subset of \(X\). Then \(S\) is said to be
  1. an open subset of \(X\), or open in \(X\) iff \[ S \cap \partial S = \emptyset \]
  2. a closed subset of (X\), or closed in \(X\) iff \[ \partial S \subseteq S \]

Note that most subsets of metric spaces are neither open nor closed.

Topologies

Definition: Topology Suppose \((X, d)\) is a metric space. The collection of open subsets of \(X\) is called the topology determined by the metric \(d\).\

Topologies are algebraically closed under all unions and finite intersections.

Theorem: Suppose \(X\) is a metric space and \(\mathcal{C}\) is a non-empty collection of subsets of \(X\).
  1. If each member of \(\mathcal{C}\) is closed in \(X\), then \(\cap \mathcal{C}\) is closed in \(X\).
  2. If \(\mathcal{C}\) is finite and each member of \(\mathcal{C}\) is closed in \(X\), then \(\cup \mathcal{C}\) is closed in \(X\).
  3. If each member of \(\mathcal{C}\) is open in \(X\), then \(\cup \mathcal{C}\) is open in \(X\).
  4. If \(\mathcal{C}\) is finite and each member of \(\mathcal{C}\) is open in \(X\), then \(\cap \mathcal{C}\) is open in \(X\).
