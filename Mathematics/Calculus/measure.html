Riemann Integral

Surprisingly, whenever we are calculating integrations for an expected value, entropy, or KL divergence etc in statistics, or of course, in machine learning, we are implicitly performing a Lebesgue integral that is NOT what we learned in basic calculus classes. First, we revisite the familiar integral.

Let \(f: [a, b] \to \mathbb{R} \) be a bounded function defined on a closed interval \([a, b]\).

A partition \(P\) of \([a, b]\) is a finite sequence of points: \[ P = \{x_0, x_1, x_2, \cdots, x_n\} \] where \(a = x_0 < x_1 < x_2 < \cdots < x_n = b\).

The subintervals are \([x_{i-1}, x_i]\) for \(i = 1, 2, \cdots, n\).

For each subinterval, the infimum (greatest lower bound) of \(f\) is denoted by: \[ m_i = \inf_{x\in [x_{i-1}, x_i]} f(x) \] and the supremum (least upper bound) of \(f\) is denoted by: \[ M_i = \sup_{x\in [x_{i-1}, x_i]} f(x). \]
Here, we define the lower sum: \[ L(f, P) = \sum_{i=1}^n m_i (x_i - x_{i-1}) \] and the upper sum: \[ U(f, P) = \sum_{i=1}^n M_i (x_i - x_{i-1}). \]
The lower Riemann integral of \(f\) over \([a, b]\) is \[ \underline{\int_a^b} f(x)dx = \sup_P L(f, P) \] and the upper Riemann integral of \(f\) over \([a, b]\) is \[ \overline{\int_a^b} f(x)dx = \inf_P U(f, P). \]
A function \(f\) is said to be Riemann integrable on \([a, b]\) if \[ \underline{\int_a^b} f(x)dx = \overline{\int_a^b} f(x)dx = \alpha. \] When this holds, the common value \(\alpha\) is called the Riemann integral of \(f\) over \([a, b]\): \[ \int_a^b f(x)dx = \alpha. \]
Note: Equivalently, we can use the norm of the partition \(P\): \[ \| P \| = \max_{1 \leq i \leq n} (x_i - x_{i-1}). \] then a function \(f\) is said to be Riemann integrable on \([a, b]\) if for any \(\| P \|\), \[ \lim_{ \| P \| \to 0} L(f, P) = \lim_{ \| P \| \to 0} U(f, P) = \alpha. \]
