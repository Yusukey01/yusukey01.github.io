The Compass Philosophy - Foundations & Mission

MATH-CS COMPASS is designed to bridge the gap between the rigorous logic of pure mathematics and the practical innovation of computer science.

In the modern view, mathematics is the study of structure. Pure mathematicians tend to prize formal rigor, and computer scientists to prize what works in practice; but understanding the structure underneath a system rewards both. This site offers an accessible yet principled look at the mathematical blueprints that power modern technology — always chosen so that a tool built for one purpose turns out to matter somewhere unexpected. An idea introduced in one section is often not fully repaid until another, sometimes far away: what looks at first like an aside becomes, pages later, the key to something you could not have solved without it. That quiet return of ideas is a large part of what this curriculum is.

The Two Worlds of the Compass

Everything here is anchored by Section I (Linear Algebra to Algebraic Foundations) — the Core, where the common language of the whole site is set down. It runs from the vectors and matrices of linear algebra, through the abstract structures of groups, rings, and fields, and up to the mathematics of continuous symmetry. Every later section speaks this language. From the Core, two great domains open up:

  • The Discrete World — the realm of things that can be counted and steps that can be carried out one at a time. Section IV (Discrete Mathematics & Algorithms) studies graphs, logic, and the theory of computation — including its sharpest question, of what a machine can and cannot decide at all. Two long arcs grow from it: category theory, the language that names the single pattern behind every structure-preserving map, and cryptography, whose security was upended by the quantum computer and then rebuilt on new, quantum-resistant mathematics.
  • The Continuous World — the realm of smooth change and of quantities approached in the limit. Section II (Calculus to Optimization & Analysis) carries calculus into rigorous analysis and optimization, and then spends its longer half rebuilding those tools on curved spaces — the surface of a sphere, the space of all rotations, the shapes data traces out in high dimensions. Its unifying thread is completeness: the guarantee that a space has no holes, which is what lets computation on continuous objects be principled rather than merely approximate.

The bridge between these two worlds is Section III (Probability & Statistics), where the counting of the discrete meets the integration of the continuous and the two turn out to be one idea. It is the mathematics of reasoning when the answer is uncertain — the direct foundation of how machines learn from data.

All of this converges in Section V (Machine Learning). But this is less a final destination than a high vantage point: each topic is a place from which the tools built earlier can be seen working together, and from which the deeper mathematics still ahead comes into view — which is why the curriculum so often circles back, meeting an idea first as an application and only later building it properly from below. The clearest view reached this way is the geometry of learning itself — the principle that a good architecture is one built to respect the symmetries of its data — a path that can now be followed end to end. Other views, such as a compositional account of learning drawn from category theory, are only beginning to take shape.

Specific tools, libraries, and frameworks age quickly; the mathematical foundations beneath them do not. The aim of this site is to equip you not merely to use today's technology as a black box, but to understand, adapt, and even create new approaches as the field moves on — long after the particular libraries and techniques of any current curriculum have been replaced.
