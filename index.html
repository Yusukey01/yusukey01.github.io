The Compass Philosophy - Foundations & Mission

MATH-CS COMPASS is designed to bridge the gap between the rigorous logic of pure mathematics and the practical innovation of computer science.

In the modern view, mathematics is the study of structure. While pure mathematicians often prioritize formal rigor and computer scientists tend to focus on experimental results, I believe that understanding the underlying structure of a system enriches both perspectives. MATH-CS COMPASS offers an accessible yet principled look at the mathematical blueprints that power modern technology. Definitions planted in one section are deliberately repaid in another — a theorem stated in Section I may wait for its key ingredient until deep in Section II. Much of the Compass is quietly built around this cross-section foreshadowing and payoff.

The Two Worlds of the Compass

Your journey here is anchored by Linear Algebra to Algebraic Foundations (Section I) — a Core that now runs from vector spaces and matrix analysis through groups, rings, and fields, up to Lie theory and representation theory, and on to the algebraic backbone of quantum computation and lattice-based cryptography. From this Core, two distinct conceptual domains open up:

  • The Discrete World — The realm of countable structures and logical reasoning. Discrete Mathematics & Algorithms (Section IV) explores graph theory, combinatorics, the theory of computation, formal logic, and algebraic topology — from algorithmic complexity and proof assistants to simplicial complexes and discrete Hodge theory. Recent growth centers on two arcs: an expanding category theory series (categories, functors, adjunctions, and limits — the language of compositional structure) and a complete cryptography arc running from classical cryptography through Shor's algorithm to post-quantum lattice schemes. Here, problems are solved through precise, step-by-step logic.
  • The Continuous World — The realm of smooth change and infinite processes. Calculus to Optimization & Analysis (Section II) covers optimization, metric topology, Fourier analysis, measure theory, and an extensive functional analysis block — and now culminates in an extended course on smooth manifolds, from tangent spaces and vector fields through differential forms, orientations, integration, and Riemannian geometry. This is the analytical and geometric machinery for the spaces where modern algorithms live and converge — and on which symmetry groups act.

The bridge between these realms is Probability & Statistics (Section III), where discrete counting meets continuous distributions, translating structure into the language of uncertainty — now grounded in measure-theoretic probability and extending into stochastic processes.

Machine Learning (Section V) is where these foundations meet real-world application. But rather than a final destination, each topic in Section V serves as a viewpoint — a vantage point that reveals how mathematical tools come together, and simultaneously motivates the pursuit of deeper foundations. The curriculum targets three broad application domains: Geometric Deep Learning (symmetry and manifolds), Categorical Deep Learning (compositionality and abstraction), and Quantum Computation (Hilbert spaces and unitary evolution). Each emerges naturally when the right mathematical tools are in hand — and the Geometric Deep Learning arc can now be walked end-to-end along both of its legs: the continuous leg (Lie groups → representation theory → equivariant networks) and the discrete leg (graphs → simplicial complexes → topological deep learning). The quantum viewpoint is previewed through the cryptography arc, and Categorical Deep Learning is under active construction through the category theory series.

Specific tools, libraries, and frameworks become outdated quickly — but the mathematical foundations are eternal. My goal is to equip you with the insights necessary not only to use existing technology as a "black box," but to understand, adapt, and even create new approaches as the field progresses — long after the specific libraries and techniques featured in any current curriculum have been superseded.
